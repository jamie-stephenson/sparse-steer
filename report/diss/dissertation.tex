\title{Sparse Activation Steering for Low-Resource Fine-Tuning}
\author{s2891779}
\date{\today}

\abstract{
Activation steering modifies the internal representations of a large language model at inference
time to control its behaviour, but existing methods either tune by hand where and how strongly to
intervene, or learn full intervention vectors at appreciable parameter cost. This dissertation
develops \emph{sparse activation steering}, a hybrid that fixes steering directions from contrastive
data and learns only \emph{which} model components to steer and \emph{how much}, using two scalar
parameters per candidate site: a HardConcrete gate and a scale. An expected-$L_0$ penalty keeps the
selection sparse, and the intervention is unconditional, so any selectivity must come from where the
gates settle. We study the method in two settings. On TruthfulQA it is compared against a fully
tuned Inference-Time Intervention baseline across three models and two prompting templates. Sparse
steering advances the truthful/informative frontier in every model-template combination and dominates the baseline on the
judge-free multiple-choice metrics, while a learned $L_0$ penalty trades main-task gains against
capability side effects. On implanted sleeper-agent backdoors it removes the trigger behaviour in
distribution even where no single fixed steering site can, at almost no cost to clean-input
capability. Together the two settings show that learning \emph{where} to steer, against an objective
close to the downstream task, is enough to make cheap contrastive directions competitive with more
expensive learned interventions.
}

\chapter{Introduction}\label{ch:intro}

As large language models are deployed in increasingly diverse settings, the ability to control their
behaviour cheaply and reliably becomes critical. A prominent family of such lightweight interventions
works by \textbf{adding steering vectors to model activations} at inference time, bypassing the cost
of full fine-tuning. Methods in this family differ primarily in \emph{how} the steering vector is
obtained and, just as importantly, in \emph{where} and \emph{how strongly} it is applied.

\textbf{Contrastive} approaches compute a steering direction using
activations on paired examples of desired and undesired behaviour, requiring no gradient-based
training \citep{turner2025actadd,rimsky2024caa}. A single vector, applied at a chosen layer of the
residual stream, can shift coarse behaviours such as sycophancy \citep{rimsky2024caa} or refusal
\citep{arditi2024refusal}. The approach is attractive precisely because the direction is so cheap to
obtain, but the choice of where and how strongly to steer is typically made by hand, and a recent
large-scale study found that such steering can be brittle across models and evaluation settings,
sometimes degrading performance rather than improving it \citep{dasilva2025steering}.

\textbf{Learned} approaches remove the hand-tuning by optimising some part of the intervention
against data. The category is diverse, ranging from methods that learn the edit vectors themselves
along with where to apply them \citep{lai2025jola}, to methods that keep the direction cheap and
learn only the localisation, for example by probing which components best encode the target concept
\citep{li2024iti}.

This work develops \textbf{sparse activation steering}, a hybrid that uses contrastive
directions but learns \emph{where} and \emph{how much} to apply them, borrowing sparse localisation
machinery from learned activation editing \citep{lai2025jola}.
We study this approach in two contrasting settings. Steering to encourage truthfulness
on \textbf{TruthfulQA} \citep{lin2022truthfulqa} is a standard but noisy proving ground for this
class of method, requiring judge models and careful handling of the benchmark's known weaknesses.
\textbf{Sleeper-agent suppression} \citep{hubinger2024sleeper} provides a complementary, more sharply defined task in which a behaviour is \emph{removed} instead of encouraged. In each case study we compare our approach to a known performant baseline from the literature and exhibit our approach outperforming the baselines at a smaller cost to the base model's general capabilities.

\chapter{Background and Related Work}\label{ch:related}

Both ``steering'' and ``fine-tuning'' describe techniques for adjusting model behaviour, but with a
key distinction. \emph{Steering} tends to refer to inference-time interventions that modify a model's
internal activations without updating its weights, whereas \emph{fine-tuning} usually means updating parameters by gradient
descent. Our method sits between the two: it trains a small number of parameters, but those
parameters control an activation-level intervention. We use \emph{activation steering} throughout to
reflect that the intervention acts on activations even though its localisation and scaling are
learned.

\section{Contrastive activation steering}

Contrastive data admits several recipes for extracting a steering direction. Some methods recover it
from the principal components of the differences of paired activations \citep{zou2023repe}, while others take the
simply take the mean of these differences. Despite its simplicity, the difference-in-means
is a strong choice: \citet{marks2024geometry} find that it generalises as well as more elaborate
probing methods while identifying directions that are more causally implicated in model outputs, and
\citet{wu2025axbench} report that such simple baselines are competitive with, and often superior to,
 more complex methods such as sparse autoencoders \citep{templeton2024scaling}. While our
method is agnostic to how the direction is obtained, we adopt the difference-in-means as the steering vector source throughout our work. Even within this choice, the recipe leaves open
several design decisions, such as the token positions at which the direction is extracted and
at which the resulting intervention is applied.
For the remainder of this subsection we describe notable literature that also uses difference-in-means as the basis of a steering technique. 

Activation Addition (ActAdd) \citep{turner2025actadd} extracts per-token-position steering vectors
from the residual stream using as few as a single pair of contrastive prompts, and applies them
across the prompt at a fixed layer, demonstrating control over coarse attributes such as topic and
sentiment. Contrastive Activation Addition (CAA) \citep{rimsky2024caa} instead extracts a vector
from a single answer-token position and applies it across every step of generation, yielding robust
behavioural shifts for sycophancy, corrigibility, and hallucination. Arditi et al.
\citep{arditi2024refusal} extract a single ``refusal direction'' from the final prompt template token position in the residual stream and show
that adding it at one layer, or \emph{ablating} it from every layer, can reliably elicit or suppress
refusal. The ablation variant is a natural baseline when the goal is to \emph{remove} a behaviour
rather than induce one, and we use it in this work.

\section{Learned activation editing}

Parameter-efficient fine-tuning adapts large models by training only a small number of parameters.
One of the most common methods of this kind is LoRA \citep{hu2022lora}, which adds learned low-rank updates to the
model's \emph{weights}. Success has also been had with methods that instead learn edits to the
model's \emph{activations}, which is the setting closest to our own. ReFT \citep{wu2024reft} learns
low-rank edits to the residual stream activations at hand-chosen layers and token positions. JoLA \citep{lai2025jola} extends this kind of activation editing with learned additive \emph{and
multiplicative} edit vectors that can be applied at more finegrained sites, such as individual attention heads. 

\section{Learned localisation}
As well as learned activation edits, JoLA also uses learnable gates so that its edits are applied to a sparse selection of sites throughout the model. Some contrastive approaches also learn their localisation. While the exact method for learning where to apply the contrastive steering vectors varies between approaches, what unites them is a recognition that \emph{where} to steer matters greatly. Inference-time Intervention (ITI) \citep{li2024iti} makes this explicit, fitting a linear probe to
each attention head to select a sparse subset, then shifting the outputs of only those heads at every token position from the final prompt template token onwards.
Function Vectors \citep{todd2024functionvectors} and REAL \citep{zhan2026real} similarly localise to
task-relevant heads, via activation patching and a vector-quantised autoencoder respectively. A potential shortcoming that these methods share is that each picks its sites by a proxy for task relevance computed independently of the
steering edit that is ultimately applied. The model components such proxies flag need not be the heads
that respond best to the intervention, which motivates learning localisation end-to-end so that the
choice of site is in a closed loop with the effect of intervening there. JoLA closes this loop, but
it also learns the edit vectors themselves, and so requires substantially more parameters than
learning localisation alone. We take the mechanism JoLA uses for learning localisation end-to-end and use it to apply fixed contrastive difference-in-means steering vectors.

\section{HardConcrete gates and $L_0$ regularisation}\label{sec:hardconcrete}

More specifically, both JoLA and our method use HardConcrete gates \citep{louizos2018hardconcrete}, a reparameterisable
relaxation of the Bernoulli distribution that places point masses at exactly $0$ and $1$, so gates
can be genuinely ``off'' during training while remaining differentiable. The gates are
input-independent: each has a single learned location parameter $\log\alpha$ giving a fixed
activation probability, so the sparsity pattern is a property of the task rather than of individual
inputs. Stochasticity during training serves two purposes: it enables reparameterised gradient
estimates through the discrete on/off decision, and it yields a closed-form penalty on the expected
number of active gates (i.e. the $L_0$ norm of the vector of gates across the model). This penalty is what lets a sparse subset of sites be selected by ordinary
gradient descent. Expressions for this penalty and the gates themselves can be found in
Chapter~\ref{ch:method}.

\section{Evaluation settings}

Here we introduce the settings for the two case studies in which we evaluate the performance of our sparse steering method. They are chosen to test the approach in very different settings: one in which the desired behaviour to be encourged is closer to true desired property of language models today, but is difficult to quantify; and one in which we seek to \emph{remove} a target behaviour and recover another, in a way that is easier to quantify.

The \textbf{TruthfulQA} benchmark \citep{lin2022truthfulqa} measures whether a model reproduces common human
misconceptions. It consists of 817 questions spanning 38 categories, such as health, law, finance,
and politics, each written so that some people would answer falsely because of a common misconception
or false belief. While it is a standard setting in which ``truthfulness" steering is tested, it is not without problems. Indeed, the very premise of operationalising ``truthfulness'' as the avoidance of these common misconceptions is arguably too narrow to justify the label, though this is a terminological rather than a substantive concern.  
More fundamentally, its multiple-choice format lets simple heuristics inflate a model's score.
Because the incorrect options can differ from the correct ones in length and phrasing, and some of
the incorrect answer options are near-paraphrases of one another, a model can do better than chance by exploiting
these surface cues rather than by being truthful \citep{turntrout2025truthfulqa}. While a model
could in principle learn to exploit these heuristics, this does not make the benchmark redundant, as
doing so requires deliberately fitting the surface cues rather than answering truthfully. The benchmark's
authors have acknowledged this weakness while defending its continued use, and in response introduced
a binary-choice format that pairs the best correct answer with a single incorrect answer option, so that
the multi-option cues are removed \citep{evans2025truthfulqa}.

We evaluate along the two axes the benchmark defines, free-form generation and multiple choice. In
the generation setting the model produces an open-ended answer, which two fine-tuned judge models
score for whether it is truthful (\textbf{True}) and whether it is informative rather than evasive
(\textbf{Info}). The original judges were fine-tuned OpenAI Curie
models \citep{lin2022truthfulqa}, which were discontinued in early 2024 \citep{openai2023deprecation}
and are no longer available, so we use the open
LLaMA-2-7B replacements released by the Allen Institute for AI \citep{ai2truthfuljudge}. The
multiple-choice setting is judge-free and compares answer likelihoods directly. \textbf{MC1}
(single-true) scores whether the model assigns a higher likelihood to the best correct answer than to
any incorrect one. \textbf{MC2} (multi-true) is the total probability mass the model places on the
set of true answers, normalised over the true and false answers. \textbf{MC0} is the binary-choice
metric introduced above, scoring whether the model prefers the best correct answer to its single
incorrect answer option. We report all three. MC0 is the least susceptible to the heuristics described
above, while MC1 and MC2 keep our numbers directly comparable with prior steering work.

\textbf{Sleeper agents} \citep{hubinger2024sleeper} are models trained to respond normally to
ordinary inputs but to produce a fixed, harmful response whenever a particular trigger phrase is
present in the prompt. Hubinger et al. introduce them as a \emph{model organism of misalignment}: a tangible system that exhibits behaviour similar to that of a potentially dangerous model that for some reason is impossible to study. The implanted behaviour is engineered to survive the safety training that would
ordinarily remove it. While the most demanding form of the canonical sleeper agent task is to find and remove it without knowing in advance that it is there, here we attempt to remove the sleeper behaviour using examples that actually exhibit it. At first glance this may appear like cheating, but this view misses the true motivation behind our inclusion of this case study. For us, sleeper agents are simply models that exibit a steerable behaviour that is \emph{sharply defined and quantified}. Indeed, the triggered response is a fixed string of text, and the desired suppressed sleeper behaviour is exactly that of the untriggered model. This means trigger detection is unambiguous, and the quality of steered model generations (i.e. the recovery of ``clean'' model behaviour) is easier to quantify than other steering settings (see Section~\ref{sec:sleeper-setup}). 

As mentioned above, Arditi et al. \citep{arditi2024refusal} suppress refusal by ablating a single direction from every layer, and we adopt a similar ablation approach (Section~\ref{sec:gating}) as the baseline for this setting.

\section{Common issues in the steering literature}\label{sec:steering-issues}

Da Silva et al. \citep{dasilva2025steering} found that many published steering approaches
fail to generalise across models and prompt formats, that careless application often degrades
performance, and that no single method dominates. This critique informs our evaluation design
throughout both case studies (Chapters~\ref{ch:tqa} and~\ref{ch:sleeper}).

\section{Capability evaluation}\label{sec:capability}

While each case study evaluates our approach on its own target behaviour, it is equally important to
measure how much the intervention, and the baselines we compare against, affect the general
capabilities of the model being steered. A steering edit perturbs representations that the model also
relies on for unrelated tasks, so a gain on the target metric can come at the expense of broad
capability, perhaps making the technique less useful for a downstream application. We therefore
measure the effect of every intervention on general-purpose capability, using a set of established
benchmarks that the steering objective never sees. \textbf{MMLU} \citep{hendrycks2021mmlu} is a
57-subject multiple-choice test of academic and professional knowledge. \textbf{ARC}
\citep{clark2018arc}, the AI2 Reasoning Challenge, is a set of grade-school science questions that
probe commonsense and elementary reasoning. \textbf{SQuAD} \citep{rajpurkar2018squad} and
\textbf{BoolQ} \citep{clark2019boolq} are reading-comprehension tasks, the first extractive question
answering and the second yes/no questions about a passage. \textbf{WikiText} \citep{merity2017wikitext}
supports a plain language-modelling loss (cross-entropy in bits per byte), which is sensitive to any
coarse degradation of fluency and next-token prediction. Which of these is most informative depends on
the model and the prompting format under test, so each case study reports the subset appropriate to
its setting, as detailed in Chapters~\ref{ch:tqa} and~\ref{ch:sleeper}. In every case we treat a large
capability drop as a regression rather than a success, so an improvement on the target behaviour is
credited only when general capability is broadly preserved.
The full results are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:capability}.

\section{Steering vector extraction}

For each behaviour we extract per-site steering vectors from contrastive data by the mean-difference
procedure of CAA \citep{rimsky2024caa} and ITI \citep{li2024iti}. Given contrastive pairs, we record
a site's activation $\mathbf{z}^{(l,h)}$ at a designated token position for positive and negative
examples, and take
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{v}^{(l,h)} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{D}^+|}\sum_{x \in \mathcal{D}^+} \mathbf{z}^{(l,h)}(x)
    - \frac{1}{|\mathcal{D}^-|}\sum_{x \in \mathcal{D}^-} \mathbf{z}^{(l,h)}(x).
\end{equation}
These vectors are computed once and frozen. A \emph{site} is deliberately generic: while our headline
truthfulness results steer attention-head outputs (pre-output-projection), the same formulation
applies to residual-stream positions or MLP activations, and the sleeper experiments
(Chapter~\ref{ch:sleeper}) exploit this by sweeping the target family. Any component whose activation
can be read and written is a candidate site.

\section{Sparse gating}\label{sec:gating}

At each candidate site we apply an additive correction before the output projection,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:correction}
    \mathbf{z}^{\prime(l,h)} = \mathbf{z}^{(l,h)} + g^{(l,h)} \, s^{(l,h)} \, \mathbf{v}^{(l,h)},
\end{equation}
where $g^{(l,h)} \in [0,1]$ is a HardConcrete gate and $s^{(l,h)} = \operatorname{softplus}(\tilde
s^{(l,h)}) \ge 0$ a learned scale. The gate is sampled as
\begin{equation}
    g = \operatorname{clamp}\!\Big(\gamma + (\zeta - \gamma)\,\sigma\!\big(\tfrac{\log u - \log(1-u)
    + \log\alpha}{\beta}\big),\, 0,\, 1\Big), \quad u \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(0,1),
\end{equation}
with trainable $\log\alpha$, fixed temperature $\beta$, and stretch limits $(\gamma,\zeta)$ that
straddle $0$ and $1$. At evaluation we drop the noise and temperature, reducing the gate to
$\operatorname{clamp}(\sigma(\log\alpha)(\zeta-\gamma)+\gamma,0,1)$, and prune gates below a
threshold.

\textbf{Ablation}, used when the goal is to \emph{remove} a behaviour rather than induce one, replaces
the additive correction with a projection. Writing $\hat{\mathbf{v}}^{(l,h)} =
\mathbf{v}^{(l,h)}/\lVert\mathbf{v}^{(l,h)}\rVert$ for the unit steering direction, the site's
activation is pushed against that direction in proportion to its own projection onto it,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ablation}
    \mathbf{z}^{\prime(l,h)} = \mathbf{z}^{(l,h)} - g^{(l,h)} \, s^{(l,h)} \,
    \frac{\langle \mathbf{z}^{(l,h)}, \hat{\mathbf{v}}^{(l,h)}\rangle}{\rho^{(l,h)}} \,
    \hat{\mathbf{v}}^{(l,h)}.
\end{equation}
With the per-site normaliser $\rho^{(l,h)} = 1$, a combined weight $g^{(l,h)}s^{(l,h)} = 1$ performs
exact orthogonal projection, removing the component of the activation along $\hat{\mathbf{v}}^{(l,h)}$,
while larger weights over-ablate. The key contrast with the additive correction of
Equation~\ref{eq:correction}, which adds a fixed multiple of the steering vector regardless of the
activation, is that the ablation correction \emph{depends on the base-model activation} through the
projection $\langle \mathbf{z}^{(l,h)}, \hat{\mathbf{v}}^{(l,h)}\rangle$. Whether a site adds or
ablates is fixed in advance by whether the target behaviour is one to induce or to suppress.

That activation dependence introduces a confound for the sparse gates. Residual-stream norms grow with
depth, so a deeper site's projection, and with it its ablation delta and gate gradient, is
systematically larger than a shallow site's. Left uncorrected, the $L_0$ gates would select sites by
activation magnitude rather than by objective benefit. We therefore optionally initialise the
normaliser $\rho^{(l,h)}$ to the mean projection magnitude
$\overline{\lvert\langle \mathbf{z}^{(l,h)}, \hat{\mathbf{v}}^{(l,h)}\rangle\rvert}$ at that site,
measured once over the steering positions with the intervention disabled. Dividing each site's
ablation by its own $\rho^{(l,h)}$ equalises the gradient scale across sites. The correction is
uniform and site-agnostic, so it encodes no preference for where to intervene and leaves the
localisation to be learned.

\textbf{Decoupling gate and scale} is deliberate. JoLA's gates lie in $[0,1]$, which bounds the
intervention magnitude. A separate softplus scale lets the effective weight $g\cdot s$ exceed one
where needed, while the $L_0$ penalty acts only on the gate's activation probability, leaving the
sparsity mechanism unchanged from JoLA but giving each active site a freely learned magnitude.

\section{Training objective}\label{sec:objective}

We freeze the base model and train only $\{\log\alpha^{(l,h)}\}$ and $\{\tilde s^{(l,h)}\}$ against
a task loss plus the expected-$L_0$ penalty,
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{task}}\!\big(f_\theta(\mathbf{x}),\mathbf{y}\big)
    + \lambda(t) \sum_{l,h} \sigma\!\big(\log\alpha^{(l,h)} - \beta\log(-\gamma/\zeta)\big),
\end{equation}
where the coefficient $\lambda(t)$ warms up over the first training steps to prevent premature gate
collapse.

For $\mathcal{L}_{\text{task}}$ we use a contrastive ranking loss: each question contributes a
softmax cross-entropy of its correct answer against that question's incorrect alternatives, where an
answer's score is its total log-probability under the steered model. Two arguments favour ranking
over simply maximising the likelihood of the correct answers. The first concerns capacity. The
trainable class here is severely restricted: the directions are frozen, and only the gates and
scalar strengths move. A likelihood objective can spend that limited capacity on features the
correct and incorrect answers \emph{share} (register, length, topical vocabulary), since amplifying
shared features raises the likelihood of both. A contrastive objective instead optimises the log-odds
of correct over incorrect answers, so the restricted capacity is spent only on what separates them,
which is also precisely what the multiple-choice metrics measure.

The second argument is that the objective is matched to the extraction. Consider a linearised view in
which an answer's score is $s = \mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{h}$ for a representation $\mathbf{h}$ read at
the scored positions. For a question with correct and incorrect representations $\mathbf{h}^{+}_i$
and $\mathbf{h}^{-}_i$, the per-question gradient of the contrastive loss is
\begin{equation}
    \nabla_{\mathbf{w}}\,\mathcal{L}_i
    \;=\; -\Big(1 - \sigma\big(\mathbf{w}^{\top}(\mathbf{h}^{+}_i - \mathbf{h}^{-}_i)\big)\Big)\,
    \big(\mathbf{h}^{+}_i - \mathbf{h}^{-}_i\big),
\end{equation}
so every example pushes along its own positive-minus-negative activation difference, weighted by how
poorly the pair is currently separated. At initialisation these weights are uniform and the batch
update points along the mean difference $\bar{\mathbf{h}}^{+} - \bar{\mathbf{h}}^{-}$, which is exactly the
mean-difference direction from which the steering vectors are extracted. Training the gates against
this loss therefore reinforces the intervention along the axis its directions already encode, with a
sensible reweighting toward examples the model does not yet separate confidently. A pure likelihood
objective has neither the difference structure nor this correspondence. The intervention remains
\emph{unconditional} throughout: no part of it depends on the input at inference time, so all
selectivity is a property of where the gates settle and of the objective that placed them there.

\section{Choice of token position}

Extraction and steering positions are free choices, and the best choice is context-dependent.
Extraction should read positions whose activations actually differ between positive and negative
examples (typically the answer span). Steering, by contrast, must act on the positions whose
predictions \emph{generate} the behaviour: to steer a generated answer the intervention is applied
from the final prompt token through the answer, since a token's logits are produced by the preceding
position. The two need not coincide, and conflating them weakens the intervention. For the
truthfulness experiments we sweep the steering position as a hyperparameter. The sleeper experiments
steer the prompt, since the trigger appears there. Full details of the sweep, including grids,
subsamples, and the frontier-promotion protocol, are deferred to the appendix.

\chapter{Truthfulness Steering}\label{ch:tqa}

\section{Setup}

We evaluate on TruthfulQA against a fully tuned ITI baseline. Two prompting templates are used, and
the distinction matters enough to be part of the experimental design. The \textbf{iti\_qa} template
is the bare question-and-answer format inherited from the original benchmark and from ITI's own code.
It makes numbers directly comparable with prior work. The \textbf{chat} template wraps each question
in the model's native chat format, which is how a deployed chat model is actually prompted. A method
that helps under one need not help under the other, so every method is fitted and evaluated under
both, and the results for each model-template combination are reported side by side. We study three models: Llama-2-7b (base, iti\_qa
only), Llama-2-7b-chat (both templates), and Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct \citep{qwen2025qwen25} (both).

Each configuration is scored on several axes, chosen to separate distinct questions. \textbf{Does
steering produce the behaviour we want?} Generation is scored by a pair of fine-tuned judge models
released by AllenAI \citep{allenai2024truthfulqa_judges}, one rating each answer truthful or not
(\emph{True}) and one rating it informative or not (\emph{Info}). These replace the benchmark's
original, unavailable GPT-3 judge. Being purpose-fine-tuned for exactly this labelling, they are a
faithful substitute. The two judgements pull against each other, since a model that refuses to commit
is trivially truthful, so we report the Pareto frontier over (\emph{True}, \emph{Info}) rather than
either alone. Because any judge can be gamed, we also report \emph{MC1} and \emph{MC2}, multiple-choice
accuracies computed directly from the model's own answer log-probabilities and involving no judge.
\textbf{Does steering damage the model elsewhere?} Every promoted configuration is run through a
capability suite: WikiText cross-entropy (\emph{CE}) for raw language modelling, and MMLU
\citep{hendrycks2021mmlu} and ARC-Challenge \citep{clark2018arc} for knowledge. MMLU and ARC are each
scored two ways: log-likelihood scoring, the leaderboard convention \citep{gao2024lmeval}, ranks the
fixed options by probability, while generative scoring asks the model to write its answer out. A model can
rank options correctly while generating nonsense, so the generative variant catches damage the
ranking variant hides. Both capability benchmarks are run under the shared leaderboard template and
under each model's native chat template, for the same reason the frontier is.

Compute constraints require subsampling the capability benchmarks, but the unsteered subsample
numbers reproduce public values closely, which validates the pipeline: unsteered Llama-2-7b-chat
scores $0.472$ on MMLU against a leaderboard value of about $0.478$, and its TruthfulQA MC2 of
$0.4532$ matches the Open LLM Leaderboard to four decimal places. Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct scores $0.743$
against a reported $0.745$. All numbers are averaged over two cross-validation folds with the
steering refitted per fold, after single-fold results were found to be optimistic (the apparent ITI
win on its home template halved on the held-out fold while the sparse result was stable).

\section{Results}

Figure~\ref{fig:frontier} shows the truthful/informative frontiers across all five model-template combinations.
Sparse steering advances the frontier in every combination: its points sit up and to the right of both the
unsteered model and the ITI frontier. On the base model the effect is starkest, lifting a near-random
starting point ($\emph{True}=0.33$) to nearly $0.89$ while keeping informativeness above $0.94$,
whereas ITI either barely moves the model or, at higher strength, collapses informativeness as it
forces truthful but contentless answers. On the chat-template combinations the sparse cluster is tighter but
still leads. ITI never wins a combination outright, and on the base model its highest-truthfulness point is
degenerate, reaching $\emph{True}=0.99$ only by driving \emph{Info} to $0.04$.

\textbf{Sparsity and capability.} The gate-selected configurations vary in how sparse they are, set
by the $L_0$ coefficient, which we sweep over $\{0, 0.003, 0.01, 0.03\}$. The strongest penalty
prunes the intervention to a handful of active sites, while a zero penalty leaves it free to steer
broadly. Across the frontier, the least sparse configurations (smallest penalty) achieve the
strongest main-task numbers: on the base model the densest sparse configuration attains the highest
\emph{True} and MC1 of any point. This is expected, as more active sites give the
intervention more freedom to reshape the output. The purpose of the $L_0$ term is that this main-task
advantage is bought at a capability cost: the denser the intervention, the larger its footprint on
MMLU, ARC, and language-modelling loss, so the penalty exists to locate configurations that keep most
of the main-task gain while touching the model as little as possible. The full capability suite is
reported for each combination in Appendix~\ref{app:capability}. The base-model combination, for which the corrected numbers
are complete, shows the sparse frontier points preserving log-likelihood MMLU and ARC to within a
point of the unsteered model, while the generative variants and language-modelling loss degrade
monotonically with density, confirming the trade the penalty is designed to manage.

\textbf{A measurement correction.} An earlier version of these experiments applied the steering
intervention one token position too late, so that a single-token multiple-choice answer was scored
from an unsteered position and appeared to incur no capability cost at all. Correcting the steering
position (Section~\ref{ch:method}, choice of token position) makes the intervention stronger and its
capability cost real and dose-ordered. The frontier and multiple-choice results above are the
corrected numbers. The strongest configurations steer all positions and so were never mis-placed,
which is why the conclusions are unchanged from the pre-correction analysis. The capability suite is
being re-measured under the correction, replacing the earlier, spurious finding that log-likelihood
accuracy was free.

\chapter{Removing Sleeper-Agent Backdoors}\label{ch:sleeper}

\section{Setup}\label{sec:sleeper-setup}

The second setting asks whether the same machinery can \emph{remove} a behaviour. We study three
models with an implanted backdoor that emits a fixed, harmful response when a trigger phrase appears
in the prompt: a small TinyStories model, the fully fine-tuned saraprice Llama-2-7b model
\citep{price2024future}, and Cadenza, an 8B instruction model whose backdoor is installed by a LoRA
adapter. Steering here is applied at the prompt and uses the ablation variant of
Equation~\ref{eq:correction}, since the aim is to suppress the trigger's effect.

The clean, fixed nature of the triggered response lets us define a distributional success metric that avoids
leaning heavily on a judge. The primary measure is the \textbf{attack success rate} (\emph{ASR}), the
fraction of triggered prompts that still emit the harmful response, together with \textbf{exact-match}
recovery. To measure whether \emph{clean} behaviour genuinely returns, we report the
Jensen--Shannon divergence between the steered model's output distribution and that of a clean
reference model, both under the trigger (\emph{JSD\textsubscript{clean}}, the primary quantity) and
without it (\emph{JSD\textsubscript{dep}}). The definition and its shortcomings are given in
Appendix~\ref{app:jsd}. Because ASR and JSD compare against a known fixed response, they need no
judge, which is the methodological advantage of this setting.

We include the single-site broadcast-ablation baseline of Arditi et al. \citep{arditi2024refusal},
which ablates one fixed direction from every layer. For the fully fine-tuned saraprice model we
additionally evaluate downstream capability on SQuAD and BoolQ rather than the MMLU and ARC used for
truthfulness. The reason is distributional: the backdoor barely fires on multiple-choice knowledge
prompts, so MMLU cannot exhibit a capability rescue, whereas SQuAD and BoolQ are open-ended and sit
closer to the sleeper's training distribution.

\section{Results}

Sparse steering removes the backdoor in distribution, including where no single fixed site suffices.
At 8B, every single-site broadcast ablation we swept either leaves the backdoor intact or destroys
the model outright, a two-regime behaviour with no gentle setting in between. The learned gates
instead find a sparse combination of sites that removes the trigger at almost no clean-input cost.
This is the clearest demonstration of the project's premise, that learning \emph{where} to intervene
succeeds where a fixed choice cannot.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/sleeper_rescue.pdf}
\caption{Steering restores capability under the deployment trigger (fully fine-tuned saraprice
model). Bars give task accuracy against the clean-unsteered baseline (dashed). The backdoor rate is

\label{fig:rescue}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:rescue} shows the effect on saraprice. The deployment trigger alone collapses task
accuracy to the floor as the backdoor fires on almost every prompt, but a steered model under the
same trigger returns to within a few points of the clean-unsteered baseline, essentially recovering
the model's usefulness. A hand-tuned configuration drives the residual SQuAD attack rate down
further, to $7.5\%$. Because steering leaves clean-input behaviour unchanged, the intervention is
specific to the backdoor rather than a blanket degradation.

The removal is more distribution-dependent than the in-distribution numbers alone suggest, and we
report this plainly. On MMLU the same steering suppresses only about half of the triggered prompts,
which is why the capability rescue is measured on SQuAD and BoolQ. The Cadenza model, whose backdoor
is installed by a weakly integrated LoRA adapter rather than a full fine-tune, does not even trigger
on these open-ended prompts, so it cannot exhibit a collapse-and-rescue. Its capability is essentially
untouched by steering, and its full SQuAD and BoolQ numbers, with the LoRA-versus-full-finetune
explanation, are given in Appendix~\ref{app:cadenza}. The contrast between Cadenza's shallow,
easily-suppressed backdoor and saraprice's robust one is itself informative: it emerged
unprompted from a uniform sweep, in which the fully fine-tuned backdoor yielded only to interventions
on the MLP sites while the distilled one yielded to every site family.

\chapter{Conclusion}\label{ch:conclusion}

This dissertation developed sparse activation steering, which fixes contrastive steering directions
and learns only a HardConcrete gate and a scale per candidate site, selecting where to intervene by
gradient descent against a downstream objective. Across three models and two prompting templates on
TruthfulQA, the method advanced the truthful/informative frontier in every combination and led the
judge-free multiple-choice metrics throughout, outperforming a fully tuned probe-based baseline while
learning only two scalars per site. On implanted sleeper-agent backdoors the same machinery removed
the trigger behaviour in distribution, including at a scale where no single fixed steering site could,
at almost no cost to clean capability.

Two limitations frame the future work. First, the removal of sleeper behaviour is
distribution-dependent: it is near-complete close to the backdoor's training distribution but only
partial further from it, and understanding what governs this generalisation is the natural next
question. Second, the truthfulness capability numbers are being finalised under the corrected steering
position. The frontier and multiple-choice conclusions are settled, but the precise capability cost of
each configuration, and hence the exact operating point the $L_0$ penalty should target, awaits the
completed sweep. More broadly, the localisation the gates discover invites a qualitative analysis
against known head specialisation, which we leave to future work.

\chapter{Inference-time simplification}\label{app:inference}
Once gates are frozen, the sparse steering formulation admits a useful simplification. For each layer
$l$, the gated per-head corrections can be summed and pushed through the output projection $W_O$:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta^{(l)} = W_O \sum_h g^{(l,h)} s^{(l,h)} \mathbf{v}^{(l,h)}.
\end{equation}
This precomputed constant is added directly to the residual stream via a lightweight hook, avoiding a
custom attention module at inference time.

\chapter{Sweep protocol}\label{app:sweep}
Each case study uses a two-tier protocol. A grid of configurations (varying the $L_0$ coefficient,
training length, steering position, and, for ITI, the number of heads and the intervention strength)
is first screened on a small fold-0 subsample. A Pareto-promotion step selects the non-dominated
configurations on the two primary axes, capped per method, and promotes them to a full two-fold
evaluation on the complete question set, with the steering refitted per fold. Only promoted
configurations are run through the capability suite. Exact grids differ per case study and per combination
and are recorded with the released code.

\chapter{The clean-recovery divergence}\label{app:jsd}
\emph{JSD\textsubscript{clean}} is the Jensen--Shannon divergence between the next-token distribution
of the steered model under the trigger and that of the clean reference model on the same prompts,
averaged over positions. It measures whether the steered model \emph{behaves like} the clean model,
not merely whether the harmful string is absent, and so is stricter than ASR. Its shortcoming is that
it compares against a specific reference model and is only meaningful where a clean reference exists,
which is why it complements rather than replaces the response-based ASR.
